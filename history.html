Brief History

Ancient Cryptography

Egypt (1900 BC): Early forms of cryptography appeared in Egypt with inscriptions in non-standard hieroglyphs used to encode messages on tombs and monuments. These were not necessarily secret codes but were intended to make the inscriptions appear more dignified.

Sparta (500 BC): Spartans used the scytale, a cylindrical tool that wrapped a strip of parchment around a baton to encode messages. This ensured that only those with a matching baton could decode the message.

Julius Caesar (100 BC): The Caesar Cipher, a substitution cipher where each letter in the plaintext is shifted a certain number of places down the alphabet, was used to securely transmit military messages.

Middle Ages - Renaissance

Al-Kindi (801 - 873 AD): introduces frequency analysis for breaking monoalphabetic substitution ciphers

Vigenère Cipher (1553): Introduced by Giovan Battista Bellaso and later attributed to Blaise de Vigenère, this cipher used a keyword to determine the shift of letters in the plaintext, providing stronger encryption.

19th Century

Enigma Machine (1918) Developed by Arthur Scherbius, the Enigma Machine was used extensively by Germany during World War II. It utilized interchangeable rotors to encrypt messages. However, it was eventually cracked by Polish codebreakers and British mathematician Alan Turing, significantly aiding the Allied war effort.

Modern Cryptography

RSA Algorithm (1977): Developed by Ron Rivest, Adi Shamir, and Leonard Adleman, RSA is a public key cryptosystem still in use today. It relies on the mathematical difficulty of factoring large prime numbers.

Advanced Encryption Standard (AES, 2001): AES replaced DES and remains a robust symmetric encryption standard, capable of withstanding attacks from modern computing power.
